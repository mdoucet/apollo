APOLLO LANDER — FLIGHT MANUAL

This simulator models the final phase of the Apollo Lunar Module (LM) descent, known as Program 66 (P66). During P66, the astronaut takes semi-manual control for the last ~150 meters to the lunar surface.

1. THE TWO TIMING LOOPS

The simulation runs two nested timing loops, matching how the real Apollo Guidance Computer (AGC) operated:

Fast Loop — DAP Cycle (0.1 seconds, 10 Hz)

The Digital Autopilot (DAP) runs every 0.1 seconds. Each "tick" of the game corresponds to one DAP cycle. This is where moment-to-moment control happens:

  1. Read pilot inputs — your keyboard presses are sampled
  2. Update attitude target — RHC deflections are integrated into the desired orientation
  3. Update descent rate target — ROD switch clicks adjust the target sink rate
  4. Compute thrust — a PI controller calculates engine throttle to track the target descent rate
  5. Advance physics — the state vector (position, velocity, mass) is updated using a 4th-order Runge-Kutta integrator
  6. Render frame — the canvas redraws with the new state

In the real AGC, this was the T6 RUPT — a hardware interrupt that fired every ~100 ms to run the DAP code (see SERVICER.agc).

In the browser, setInterval(tick, 100) drives this loop. Each tick sends your current keyboard state to the server, which runs one env.step(), and the result is rendered on canvas.

Slow Loop — Guidance Cycle (2.0 seconds, 0.5 Hz)

Every 2.0 seconds (every 20th DAP cycle), the full P66 guidance equations are recalculated. This is a heavier computation that updates the overall descent strategy:

  • Recalculate trajectory constraints
  • Update gravity compensation terms
  • Recompute the throttle bias (FWEIGHT)

In the real AGC, this was the SERVICER READACCS task, stored as 2SECS = DEC 200 (in centiseconds) in SERVICER.agc. The related constant 4SECS = DEC 400 (2 × guidance period) was used in the P66 FWEIGHT calculation.

2. CREW vs. COMPUTER: WHO CONTROLS WHAT

In Program 66, the division of labor between the AGC and the Commander was very specific. The AGC automated some tasks, but the astronaut had to manually handle others — and those manual tasks were what made landing on the Moon so challenging.

What the AGC Controlled (Automated)

Throttle The RODCOMP algorithm computed DPS thrust to maintain the commanded descent rate. The astronaut never touched the throttle directly. (Source: LUNAR_LANDING_GUIDANCE_EQUATIONS.agc pp.816-819)
Attitude hold When the RHC was released to center detent, the DAP fired opposing RCS jets to freeze the spacecraft at its current attitude. This is RCAH — Rate Command / Attitude Hold.

What the Commander Controlled (Manual)

Horizontal steering The CDR watched the LPDT cross-pointers (forward/lateral velocity from the landing radar) and manually steered with the RHC to null horizontal velocity and avoid obstacles. The AGC computed VHORIZ for display but did NOT act on it.
Descent rate The CDR clicked the ROD switch at specific altitudes to progressively slow the sink rate: −5 ft/s high up → −3 mid → −2 low → −1 ft/s for final approach. Each click changed the AGC's VDGVERT register by exactly 1 ft/s.
Landing site The CDR could pitch forward/back to redesignate the landing point. Armstrong famously did this on Apollo 11 to avoid a boulder field, burning extra fuel in the process.

Play Modes

The three modes map directly to this crew/computer division of labor:

ModeCommandYou controlComputer controls
Manual apollo play RHC + ROD (full astronaut experience) Throttle + attitude hold
Assisted apollo assisted RHC only (horizontal steering) Throttle + attitude hold + ROD scheduling

Tip: Start with assisted mode to learn how horizontal steering works. Once you're comfortable nulling velocity with the RHC, switch to manual mode for the full astronaut experience — you'll also need to manage your own descent rate schedule.

3. CONTROLS

Rotational Hand Controller (RHC)

The RHC commands attitude rates, not absolute angles. While you hold a key, the LM rotates at up to 20°/s (MAX_ROTATION_RATE = 0.349 rad/s) on that axis. When you release the key, the DAP holds the current attitude.

KeyAxisEffectRHC Value
↑PitchPitch forward (nose down)−1.0
↓PitchPitch back (nose up)+1.0
←RollRoll left−1.0
→RollRoll right+1.0
QYawYaw left−1.0
EYawYaw right+1.0

The RHC deflection (±0.5 in this sim) is multiplied by the max rotation rate each DAP cycle. So holding ↑ for one tick rotates the target attitude by: 1.0 × 0.349 × 0.1 ≈ 0.035 rad ≈ 2° per tick.

On the real LM, the RHC was a pistol-grip stick between the two astronauts. The commander used it with his right hand while looking out the left window.

Rate of Descent (ROD) Switch

The ROD switch adjusts your target descent rate in discrete 1 ft/s (0.3048 m/s) increments. This is the setpoint that the AGC's throttle controller tries to maintain.

KeyEffectResult
PgUp Decrease sink rate (−1 ft/s) Descent gets slower — target rate becomes less negative
PgDn Increase sink rate (+1 ft/s) Descent gets faster — target rate becomes more negative
Example: If your target descent rate is −5 ft/s (−1.52 m/s) and you tap PgUp twice, the new target becomes −3 ft/s (−0.91 m/s). The AGC automatically adjusts the DPS throttle to slow your descent to this rate.

Initial rate: P66 starts with a target of −5 ft/s (−1.524 m/s). You'll want to gradually decrease this to about −1 ft/s as you approach the surface.

On the real LM, the ROD switch was a toggle on the commander's armrest. Each click up or down changed the VDGVERT register in the AGC by exactly 1 ft/s, as defined by the RODSCAL1 constant in LUNAR_LANDING_GUIDANCE_EQUATIONS.agc.

4. HOW CONTROLS INTERACT WITH TIMING

Your inputs are sampled at the DAP rate (10 Hz), not the guidance rate. This means:

  • RHC is continuous: Hold a key and the attitude changes smoothly, ~1° per tick (0.1s). The DAP integrates your stick position every cycle.
  • ROD is discrete: Each key press changes the target by exactly 1 ft/s. The throttle PI controller then adjusts engine power every DAP cycle (0.1s) to converge on the new target.
  • Guidance runs in the background: Every 2 seconds, the P66 guidance loop recalculates trajectory parameters. Between guidance cycles, the DAP maintains thrust based on the most recent guidance solution.
Pilot presses PgUp at t=0.0 │ ├─ t=0.0 DAP cycle: target_descent_rate += 0.3048 m/s (1 ft/s slower) ├─ t=0.1 DAP cycle: PI controller adjusts throttle toward new rate ├─ t=0.2 DAP cycle: throttle continues converging... ├─ ... ├─ t=2.0 GUIDANCE: full P66 recalc, updates gravity compensation ├─ t=2.1 DAP cycle: uses updated guidance terms ├─ ...

5. THE DSKY: VERB AND NOUN

The DSKY (Display and Keyboard, pronounced "dis-key") was the astronaut's interface to the Apollo Guidance Computer. It used a Verb-Noun command language — similar to how English sentences have an action (verb) and a subject (noun).

Verb-Noun System

The astronaut would key in a two-digit Verb and a two-digit Noun to tell the AGC what to do with what data. For example:

  • VERB 06 = "Display (decimal)" → show me data
  • VERB 16 = "Monitor (routinely)" → keep showing me this data, updating continuously
  • VERB 37 = "Change program" → switch to a different program
  • VERB 50 = "Please perform" → AGC asks the crew to do something

Nouns specified which data to display or act on:

  • NOUN 43 = Geographic position (lat, lon, alt)
  • NOUN 62 = Velocity components
  • NOUN 68 = Landing parameters (alt, alt-rate, descent-rate)

V16 N68 — What You See During P66

During the final descent, the DSKY automatically displays Verb 16, Noun 68:

  • Verb 16 = "Monitor routinely" — the DSKY updates these values continuously without astronaut interaction
  • Noun 68 = "Landing data" — the three most critical numbers for landing
RegisterContentUnitsExample
R1 Altitude above the surface Feet × 100 +01500 = 15.00 ft
R2 Vertical velocity (altitude rate) Feet/s × 100 −00312 = −3.12 ft/s (descending)
R3 Commanded descent rate (ROD setpoint) Feet/s × 100 −00500 = −5.00 ft/s target

Reading the DSKY: The ±XXXXX format shows values scaled by 100. To get the actual value in feet or ft/s, divide the displayed number by 100.

Example: If R1 shows +04921, your altitude is 4921 ÷ 100 = 49.21 feet above the surface.

Why ×100? The AGC used fixed-point arithmetic with no floating point. Scaling by 100 gave two decimal places of precision using only integer registers.

PROG — Program Number

The PROG display shows the currently running program. During landing, this shows 66 — Program 66, the semi-automatic landing phase where the astronaut controls attitude and descent rate while the AGC manages the throttle.

6. LANDING CRITERIA

For a successful touchdown, your velocities and terrain must be within these limits when the LM reaches the surface:

ParameterLimitNotes
Vertical velocity ≤ 1.2 m/s (≈ 4 ft/s) LM gear designed for up to 7 ft/s; 4 ft/s provides margin
Horizontal velocity ≤ 0.6 m/s (≈ 2 ft/s) Prevents tipping; gear absorbs some lateral motion
Terrain slope ≤ 12° LM footpad span ~14 ft; slopes beyond 12° risk toppling

Apollo 11 actual touchdown: ~0.9 m/s (3 ft/s) vertical. Armstrong was nearly out of fuel — about 25 seconds of hover time remaining.

Failure modes:

  • CRASH — Touched down too fast or on too steep a slope
  • FUEL EXHAUSTED — DPS propellant depleted before touchdown
  • TIMEOUT — Exceeded 300 seconds (3000 DAP cycles) of flight time

7. FLIGHT TIPS

  1. Start by reducing horizontal velocity. You begin with ~5 m/s lateral motion. Use pitch (↑/↓) to steer toward the landing pad.
  2. Gradually slow your descent. The initial −5 ft/s is good for high altitude. Tap PgUp to reduce to −3 ft/s around 30m, then −1 ft/s below 10m.
  3. Watch your fuel. The DPS consumes ~14.8 kg/s at full thrust. You have about 8,200 kg of fuel — enough for ~9 minutes, but P66 starts partway through powered descent.
  4. Small corrections only. The DAP responds every 0.1s. Gentle, brief key taps are better than holding keys down.
  5. Use the HUD and DSKY together. The HUD (left) shows metric units in real-time. The DSKY (right) shows the same data in Apollo flight units (ft × 100). Cross-reference both.
